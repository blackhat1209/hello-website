Introduction to Quantum Physics

"If quantum mechanics hasn't profoundly shocked you, you haven't understood it yet." – Niels Bohr

Quantum physics studies how matter and energy behave at extremely small scales such as atoms, electrons, and photons.

In our everyday world, objects follow classical physics described by Newton. But when scientists began studying atoms, they discovered that these rules no longer worked correctly.

Particles at tiny scales behave in strange ways. Understanding these behaviours led to the development of quantum mechanics.

Why Quantum Physics Was Needed

Around the early 1900s, scientists observed several phenomena that classical physics could not explain.

  • Blackbody radiation
  • The photoelectric effect
  • The stability of atoms
  • Atomic spectral lines

These problems led physicists like Max Planck, Albert Einstein, Niels Bohr, and Erwin Schrödinger to develop quantum theory.

Atomic Structure

Atoms are made of a tiny nucleus containing protons and neutrons, with electrons surrounding it.

In classical models, electrons were thought to orbit the nucleus like planets orbit the Sun.

However, quantum mechanics shows that electrons do not travel in fixed paths. Instead, they exist in regions of probability called orbitals.

Wave–Particle Duality

One of the most surprising discoveries of quantum physics is that particles such as electrons and photons behave both like particles and like waves.

Examples:

  • Light can behave like particles called photons.
  • Electrons can produce interference patterns like waves.

This dual behaviour is known as wave–particle duality.

The Double Slit Experiment

The double slit experiment is one of the most famous demonstrations of quantum behaviour.

When particles pass through two narrow slits, they produce an interference pattern similar to waves.

Even more strangely, when scientists try to observe which slit the particle passes through, the interference pattern disappears.

This experiment reveals the mysterious nature of quantum mechanics.

Why Quantum Physics Is Important

Quantum physics is the foundation of many modern technologies.

  • Lasers
  • Semiconductors
  • Transistors
  • Quantum computing
  • MRI scanners

Without quantum mechanics, modern electronics and computers would not exist.

Question: What does wave–particle duality mean?

Wave–particle duality means particles such as electrons and photons can behave both like waves and like particles depending on the experiment being performed.
